The Australian school year

Runs February to December, not April to March. Four terms.

A2
The Indian-Australian Parent You'll Teach
~900,000 India-born Aussies. Here's what makes this community distinct.
›
Most parents you will teach in Australia are from the Indian-Australian community. Understanding who they are and what they expect is the highest-leverage context for your trial confidence.

What this community is like — the inference first

Highly educated professionals who value education deeply
They expect substance, professionalism, and specificity — not sales talk. They want to know the teacher's background, see visible progress, and feel their child's academic time is respected. Many grew up with Indian maths rigour and expect more than school-level work.
Their children are usually second generation — Australian-educated, Indian-home-raised
The child is a near-native English speaker with a bilingual home. They are typically strong on computational drill but often weaker on word problems, reasoning, and conceptual depth — which is exactly what NAPLAN, OC, and Selective tests measure. Show this gap in the trial; many parents don't yet see it.
They take academics very seriously and respect the teacher
Expect engaged, prepared parents who are sitting in on the trial and may ask direct questions. They usually respect the teacher and respond well to clear, confident feedback about the child's strengths and gaps.

What to do and not do

  • Expect rigour — go beyond school-level work during the trial
  • Be substantive and specific. Name topics. Show depth.
  • Respect the parent's experience and education — speak as a professional to a professional
  • Demonstrate progress visibly within the session
  • Don't oversell "fun" at the cost of substance
  • Don't assume they know the Australian test system — newer migrants often don't
  • Don't default to "basics only" if the child is capable of more
  • Don't make sales-style claims; let your teaching speak
Vs. native Australian parents: If you are teaching a non-Indian Australian family, adapt — they tend to be more relaxed about academic rigour, more focused on enjoyment and confidence-building, and less likely to chase competitive exams.
▸ Tap here to see the detailed demographics and sub-community breakdown

Population size — approximately 900K+ India-born Aussies (ABS, June 2024) and 800K+ with Indian ancestry (2021 Census). India is the second-largest overseas-born group. The community has grown ~35% since 2021 and has the youngest average age (34) of any migrant group in Australia.

Education levels — more than half of Indian-Australian adults hold a bachelor's degree or higher, which is more than three times the Australian national average.

Where they live — concentrated in NSW (largest), Victoria, Queensland, Western Australia, and South Australia, in that order.

Sub-community composition — Hindu is the largest religion (~half), then Christian, Sikh, and Muslim. Linguistically, the community is diverse: Hindi, Punjabi (6th most spoken language in Australia), Tamil, Malayalam, Telugu, Bengali, Gujarati, and Marathi are all well represented. Knowing the sub-community helps you pick up cultural cues in names and references — but do not assume or stereotype.

Sources: Indian Australians — Wikipedia (2021 Australian Census + June 2024 ABS update) · ABS Cultural Diversity

What the Australian Curriculum emphasises

  • Understanding over procedure. The Australian Curriculum explicitly calls out "understanding, fluency, reasoning, and problem-solving" as the four proficiency strands. Procedural fluency alone does not meet the standard.
  • Word problems and context. A large portion of assessments — including NAPLAN — are word problems, not bare calculations.
  • Visualising mathematics. The Australian Curriculum encourages diagrams, manipulatives, and visual models — which aligns strongly with the Cuemath approach.

What this means for the trial

  • Ask early: "What state are you in?" — then select the correct curriculum on screen. Let the parent see you do it.
  • If the parent asks about alignment: "Our program maps directly to the [NSW / Victorian / Western Australian / Australian] Curriculum. For Year [X], we cover [name 2-3 specific topics]."
  • Frame Cuemath's depth as matching the Australian Curriculum's emphasis on understanding: "The Australian Curriculum asks for understanding, fluency, reasoning, and problem-solving. We go deeper on exactly those four things."

The 5 Parent Archetypes You'll Meet
Based on analysis of 288 real Australian trials
›
Every parent you meet fits roughly into one of five archetypes. Spotting the archetype in the first 5 minutes of the trial helps you tune everything that follows — your tone, your examples, what to demonstrate, which goals to name back.

Tap any card below to see your play

🎯 The NAPLAN Mum
32% of AU parents · 14% convert
"NAPLAN is next year. We want to prepare from now."
Your playName the test date in your own language. Show a NAPLAN-style reasoning problem naturally during the learning session. Mention the NAPLAN Test Prep module with mock papers. Reference specific strands — measurement, fractions, problem-solving — that align with what NAPLAN tests.
🔄 The Switcher
7% of parents · 24% convert (highest)
"He was doing Kumon but I felt it was too repetitive."
Your playThey have already decided they need a tutor — they are picking one. Acknowledge what the other tool did well. Then differentiate calmly on 1-on-1 format, conceptual understanding, and the personalised learning plan. Do not bash the competitor — just contrast.
⚠️ The Panic Parent
~10% of parents · 15%+ convert
"The school just told us she's six months behind. I don't know how to take this."
Your playReassure first. Validate the worry — do not minimise it. Show you can diagnose the specific gap in the trial with clear observations. Share a realistic timeline (8–12 sessions for a visible shift). Make them feel the path is already mapped.
🏆 The OC/Selective Chaser
12% of parents · 20% convert (NSW-heavy)
"My final aim is for him to go to selective school."
Your playSpeak the specific test language fluently — OC, Selective, Thinking Skills, Mathematical Reasoning. Connect the reasoning problems you teach back to what the test rewards. Mention the Test Prep module. Name the date urgency if the test is within 12 months.
🕉️ The NRI Returner
30%+ of parents · 10% convert
"We want to keep the Indian maths rigour while following the Australian curriculum."
Your playBicultural framing. Acknowledge the Indian maths strength they grew up with. Show how Cuemath bridges both — conceptual depth paired with state curriculum alignment. Reassure that their child will not fall behind on either front.

How to spot the archetype quickly: In the first 3 minutes of goal-setting, listen for tests named, competitors named, urgency phrases, and cultural cues. The archetype usually reveals itself within 5 minutes.

Archetypes aren't rigid. A parent can be 70% Switcher and 30% Panic Parent. Use the dominant signal to guide your tone, but stay flexible. If they pivot to a new concern mid-call, follow them there.

B2
Probing the Hidden Goal
When a parent says "just overall improvement" — probe, don't pitch.
›
~25% of parents arrive without a clear goal. They say things like "just overall improvement" or "we want to start extra practice." A trial built around that goal will feel generic — and won't convert. Your job is to surface the real goal respectfully, before the session begins.

The 3-question ladder

Ask one question at a time. Listen fully. The answer to each question shapes the next.

Question 1 · The wish
"If maths was going really well for [child] in 6 months, what would that look like — in your mind?"
Opens space. Lets the parent paint a picture without feeling interrogated. Listen for: specific tests mentioned, specific grades, "confident", "faster", "ahead of class".
Question 2 · The worry
"What's the biggest thing that made you book this trial today?"
Surfaces the trigger. Often the trigger is a recent event: a school report, a NAPLAN result, another parent's comment, a test announcement. This tells you urgency and emotional weight.
Question 3 · The deadline
"Is there anything specific coming up for [child] this year or next — a test, a school application, anything on the horizon?"
Tests the timeline. Even if they said "no goal" earlier, this often pulls out: "Actually, NAPLAN is next year..." or "Selective test is in May..." Now you know what to anchor the trial to.

Do / Don't

  • Ask one probe question at a time, not all three at once
  • Let silence sit after you ask — parents often add more unprompted
  • Echo back what you heard: "So you're hoping she's confident with fractions by end of year"
  • Write their exact words down. Reference them in your summary.
  • Rattle off three questions in 30 seconds
  • Assume goals from age/grade alone
  • Sell before you've heard the worry
  • Dismiss a vague goal as "no goal" — it's usually a hidden goal
Parent at goal-setting says: "We just want him to do a bit of extra maths. No specific goal. Just keep him exposed." How do you respond?

Think about what you'd say before revealing the approach.

Don't accept the vague goal — the session will feel directionless if you do. Say: "Of course, that's a great start. I want to make today's session really count for him — if everything went beautifully over the next 6 months, what would you love to see? A stronger report card? More confidence with a specific topic? Getting ready for anything coming up at school?" Most parents then open up with something concrete. Internal fact: parents whose primary goal is "Exposure/Habit" convert at 0% — your job is to uncover the real goal underneath that surface answer.
Source for the 0% statistic: Internal analysis of 288 Australian trial transcripts (Dec 2025–Apr 2026).
B3
Goal-Driven vs Exploratory — two different trials
Adapt your trial depth based on parent readiness
›
One-size-fits-all trials lose the parent. A goal-driven parent wants you to get to the depth fast. An exploratory parent needs you to teach them what "good" looks like before you can sell. Reading this correctly in the first 5 minutes is everything.

Goal-driven parent — what they sound like

  • Names a specific test: "NAPLAN", "OC", "Selective", "scholarship exam"
  • References a deadline: "next year", "in 6 months", "before Year 7"
  • Mentions a competitor: "was doing Kumon", "tried Cluey"
  • Asks about fees unprompted in first 10 minutes
  • Has clear topic concerns: "struggles with fractions", "weak in word problems"

How to subtly mirror their goal (the trial flow is fixed — the language is yours)

The trial follows a standard structure — the screens, sequence, and core questions are not for you to change. What is yours is the language you wrap around it. Here is how to mirror the goal without altering the trial:

  • At goal-setting: repeat the test or timeline back to them — "OC in May next year, got it."
  • During the learning session: pick observations that connect to their goal when they arise naturally. If the child does well on a reasoning problem, say: "that kind of thinking is exactly what the OC Test assesses."
  • At Student Insights: phrase strengths and gaps in terms of their goal. "Her reading of word problems is strong — that helps a lot for NAPLAN."
  • At Learning Plan: walk through how the plan covers areas relevant to the goal they named.
  • Mention the Test Prep module when OC / Selective / NAPLAN is the goal — it's a real feature they should know about.

Exploratory parent — what they sound like

  • Vague answers to goal questions: "just extra practice", "overall improvement"
  • No deadline, no test mentioned
  • Doesn't name competitors
  • Doesn't ask about fees
  • Often first-time tutoring family, often younger children (Year K–3)

How to run the trial for an exploratory parent

  • Teach them what good maths learning looks like — the conceptual "why," the visualisations, the word-problem focus. Let them see the approach in action.
  • Narrate what you are doing gently: "Most parents don't realise that understanding the 'why' is what helps with school assessments — watch how we work through this."
  • Use probe questions (from Part B2) mid-trial to surface a hidden goal. By end of trial you will often have moved them from "exploratory" to "I want my child to be strong at X."
  • At Student Insights, give them a concrete thing to care about — a specific gap, a specific strength — so they leave the trial with a clearer view of their child.
The truth about exploratory parents: many of them convert more slowly, but they do convert. Don't rush. An exploratory parent who leaves the trial with a new understanding of their child is a higher-quality enrolment than a pressured one.
Your job in the trial is to teach and show, not to sell. The admissions team handles conversion after the trial. What you do in the trial is set up the conditions for a great enrolment — by showing the child what good learning feels like and showing the parent what their child is capable of.

C3
"What is the fee?" — handling the money question gracefully
Say "fee" not "price." Acknowledge, reassure, hand over smoothly.
›
Fee questions are natural — almost every parent thinks about cost. This is the area where teachers most often deflect or feel uncomfortable. Your job is to handle it gracefully so the parent feels heard, not dismissed, and then transition the specifics to the Admissions Manager.

One word change that sets the tone

Use "fee" not "price". "Price" feels like a product; "fee" feels like education. Australian parents pay fees for school, for music, for sport. Calling it a fee keeps it in that familiar, respectful space.

What you say when the parent asks

  1. Acknowledge calmly — do not freeze or rush. "That is a fair question."
  2. Tell them exactly when and where they will see the fee. "The fee is shown on the screen towards the end of the session. You will be able to see all the options there."
  3. Briefly describe what they are paying for — in terms of what the child just experienced, not a sales pitch. "What is included is a dedicated teacher, a personalised learning plan based on what we covered today, and daily practice on the app."
  4. Lightly mention the fee structure — three options for the parent to think about. "There are three tenure options — 3 months, 6 months, and 12 months. The longer the tenure, the higher the discount."
  5. Hand over to the Admissions Manager for specifics and payment plans. "Our Admissions Manager will walk you through the exact plan options and any current offers, and help you pick what works for your family."
Never quote specific discount percentages yourself (like "10% off" or "20% off"). Use directional language: "higher tenure → higher discount." This protects both the parent experience and the admissions team's ability to build the right plan.

Summarising Insights & Connecting to Goals
Be specific, be honest, reference what the parent told you
›
The Student Insights screen is the most important moment for the parent. They've sat through the entire session — this is where they hear what you observed. What you add with your own words makes the difference between a generic demo and a personalised assessment.

What Makes a Great Summary

  1. Be specific about the child. Not "your child did well" but "[child] solved the array problem independently — that shows strong visual reasoning."
  2. Name the gap constructively. Not "they struggled" but "fractions is an area where we can build a stronger foundation — and that's exactly what the Learning Plan focuses on."
  3. Connect back to their goals. If they mentioned OC prep: "The reasoning skills we worked on today are directly what the OC test assesses." If they wanted to fill gaps: name the specific gap you found.
  4. Be honest. If the child struggled, say so constructively. Parents appreciate honesty over flattery.
  • Name specific strengths with examples from the trial
  • Reference the parent's goals from the start
  • Give a clear, personalised recommendation
  • Walk through the Learning Plan and connect chapters to goals
  • "Your child did well overall" — too vague
  • Skip the summary and jump to timings
  • Focus only on weaknesses
  • Rush through the Learning Plan
E2
Ending with Confidence & Warmth
Timings, parent app, way forward — and leaving a lasting impression
›
A strong close leaves the parent feeling that their child's time was well spent. A weak or rushed close undoes all the good work from the session. Take your time with these final screens.

How to End Well

  1. Class Timings: Guide the parent to select days and times confidently — this is a natural next step, not a hard sell. If they hesitate, see B5.
  2. Way Forward: Highlight the three pillars — personalised sessions, regular practice on the app, and parent-tutor conference after 8 sessions. The conference shows ongoing accountability.
  3. Questions: Invite them to ask anything. Redirect pricing or difficult questions to the Admissions Manager — that's their domain.
  4. End with warmth: Thank the parent for their time, appreciate the child's effort, and express genuine enthusiasm about working with the student. The parent should leave the call feeling positive.
  • Take your time with the final screens — don't rush
  • End on a specific, positive note about the child
  • Mention the parent-tutor conference — parents value accountability
  • Be warm and genuine — this is about the child, not the sale
  • End abruptly or seem like you're in a hurry
  • Make it feel transactional
  • Try to answer pricing questions yourself
  • Forget to thank both parent and child

Remember: After the trial ends, the Admissions Manager follows up. If the parent remembers your specific observations, your connection to their goals, and your genuine enthusiasm — they are far more likely to move forward.
