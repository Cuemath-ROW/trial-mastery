A2
The Indian-Australian Parent You'll Teach
~900,000 India-born Aussies. Here's what makes this community distinct.
›
TL;DR
Most of your AU parents are from the Indian-Australian community — ~900K strong, youngest immigrant group in AU, 3× the national graduate rate. Understanding who they are and what they expect is the single highest-leverage context for trial confidence.

What this community is like — the inference first

Highly educated professionals who value education deeply
They expect substance, professionalism, and specificity — not sales talk. They want to know the teacher's background, see visible progress, and feel their child's academic time is respected. Many grew up with Indian maths rigour and expect more than school-level work.
Their children are usually second generation — Australian-educated, Indian-home-raised
The child is a near-native English speaker with a bilingual home. They are typically strong on computational drill but often weaker on word problems, reasoning, and conceptual depth — which is exactly what NAPLAN, OC, and Selective tests measure. Show this gap in the trial; many parents don't yet see it.
They take academics very seriously and respect the teacher
Expect engaged, prepared parents who are sitting in on the trial and may ask direct questions. They usually respect the teacher and respond well to clear, confident feedback about the child's strengths and gaps.

What to do and not do

  • Expect rigour — go beyond school-level work during the trial
  • Be substantive and specific. Name topics. Show depth.
  • Respect the parent's experience and education — speak as a professional to a professional
  • Demonstrate progress visibly within the session
  • Don't oversell "fun" at the cost of substance
  • Don't assume they know the Australian test system — newer migrants often don't
  • Don't default to "basics only" if the child is capable of more
  • Don't make sales-style claims; let your teaching speak
Vs. native Australian parents: If you are teaching a non-Indian Australian family, adapt — they tend to be more relaxed about academic rigour, more focused on enjoyment and confidence-building, and less likely to chase competitive exams.
▸ Tap here to see the detailed demographics and sub-community breakdown

Population size — approximately 900K+ India-born Aussies (ABS, June 2024) and 800K+ with Indian ancestry (2021 Census). India is the second-largest overseas-born group. The community has grown ~35% since 2021 and has the youngest average age (34) of any migrant group in Australia.

Education levels — more than half of Indian-Australian adults hold a bachelor's degree or higher, which is more than three times the Australian national average.

Where they live — concentrated in NSW (largest), Victoria, Queensland, Western Australia, and South Australia, in that order.

Sub-community composition — Hindu is the largest religion (~half), then Christian, Sikh, and Muslim. Linguistically, the community is diverse: Hindi, Punjabi (6th most spoken language in Australia), Tamil, Malayalam, Telugu, Bengali, Gujarati, and Marathi are all well represented. Knowing the sub-community helps you pick up cultural cues in names and references — but do not assume or stereotype.

What the Australian Curriculum emphasises

  • Understanding over procedure. The Australian Curriculum explicitly calls out "understanding, fluency, reasoning, and problem-solving" as the four proficiency strands. Procedural fluency alone does not meet the standard.
  • Word problems and context. A large portion of assessments — including NAPLAN — are word problems, not bare calculations.
  • Visualising mathematics. The Australian Curriculum encourages diagrams, manipulatives, and visual models — which aligns strongly with the Cuemath approach.

What this means for the trial

  • Ask early: "What state are you in?" — then select the correct curriculum on screen. Let the parent see you do it.
  • If the parent asks about alignment: "Our program maps directly to the [NSW / Victorian / Western Australian / Australian] Curriculum. For Year [X], we cover [name 2-3 specific topics]."
  • Frame Cuemath's depth as matching the Australian Curriculum's emphasis on understanding: "The Australian Curriculum asks for understanding, fluency, reasoning, and problem-solving. We go deeper on exactly those four things."

B2
Probing the Hidden Goal
When a parent says "just overall improvement" — probe, don't pitch.
›
TL;DR
~25% of parents arrive with a vague goal. "Just overall improvement" converts at 0% if you run a generic trial around it. Surface the real goal with 3 questions, one at a time — wish, worry, deadline.
The 3-question ladder — one question, listen, then next
Question 1 · The wish
"If maths was going really well for [child] in 6 months, what would that look like — in your mind?"
Opens space. Lets the parent paint a picture without feeling interrogated. Listen for: specific tests mentioned, specific grades, "confident", "faster", "ahead of class".
Question 2 · The worry
"What's the biggest thing that made you book this trial today?"
Surfaces the trigger. Often the trigger is a recent event: a school report, a NAPLAN result, another parent's comment, a test announcement. This tells you urgency and emotional weight.
Question 3 · The deadline
"Is there anything specific coming up for [child] this year or next — a test, a school application, anything on the horizon?"
Tests the timeline. Even if they said "no goal" earlier, this often pulls out: "Actually, NAPLAN is next year..." or "Selective test is in May..." Now you know what to anchor the trial to.
✓Do
  • Ask one question at a time, not all three at once
  • Let silence sit — parents often add more unprompted
  • Echo back what you heard: "So you're hoping she's confident with fractions by end of year"
  • Write their exact words down. Reference them in your summary.
✗Don't
  • Rattle off three questions in 30 seconds
  • Assume goals from age/grade alone
  • Sell before you've heard the worry
  • Dismiss a vague goal as "no goal" — it's usually hidden
