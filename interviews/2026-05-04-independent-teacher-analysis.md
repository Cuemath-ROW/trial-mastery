# Teacher Interviews: Independent Bottom-Up Qualitative Analysis

**Source**: 10 phone interviews (T01–T10), conducted 2026-04-29 to 2026-04-30  
**Transcription**: Whisper auto-transcription — some artifacts present, noted where relevant  
**Analyst note**: This analysis imposes no prior framework. All themes emerged from reading the transcripts cold. Quotes are reproduced as transcribed; garbled Whisper output is noted with [unclear] where it obscures meaning but does not prevent interpretation.

---

## What the transcripts are

These are phone calls between the head of the APAC and Europe business and individual teachers. The interviewer explains a research project and asks open-ended questions. Most teachers have not been briefed in advance. The format is semi-structured but conversational — the interviewer frequently pivots based on what the teacher says. Most calls run 15–25 minutes.

A structural note: because the interviewer is the head of business, teachers are talking to someone with authority over their livelihood. This shapes the conversations in ways that are worth holding in mind throughout — some teachers are unusually candid (T09, T10, T04), others are more deferential and careful (T06, T07). This is not a neutral research context.

---

## Theme 1: Teachers Can't Name What Converts — They Theorize After the Fact

Nearly every teacher, when asked what makes a trial convert, produces a post-hoc rationalization rather than a live model. The explanations are plausible but inconsistent across teachers and often internally inconsistent within the same teacher's account. No teacher spontaneously described a repeatable, deliberate trial methodology they execute consistently.

**What they say:**

- T02: "I love interacting with children... it's not just a job for me. I take it personally. Every child is a child to me." — Then separately attributes conversion to managing the parent's intervention during the class. Two different causal stories.
- T05: "Frankly speaking earlier I used to [prepare] but now I have taken so many so I am not going to stop preparing for anything now." — Then immediately advises a hypothetical new teacher to prepare thoroughly.
- T10: "My majority of the cases I have handled is win-win for me" — then later concedes "maybe you got lucky that your parents were with high intent."
- T01: "I will just observe the requirements of the parent and what they expect... that will be my first observation." — But when asked if any trial converted, none had.

**Who showed it**: T01, T02, T03, T05, T06, T07, T08, T09, T10 — essentially universal.

**Counter-evidence**: T04 is the most systematic of all teachers; she describes profiling parents, preparing sheets across three difficulty levels, and adjusting based on stated goals. T09 also articulates a clear priority hierarchy (rapport first, parent confidence second, teacher knowledge third). But neither can point to a consistent repeatable process either — T04's explanations of why trials fail still land on pricing.

**Nuance**: The post-hoc rationalization is not dishonesty. Teachers appear to genuinely believe their explanations. The pattern suggests that trial conversion is a tacit skill these teachers have not been helped to make explicit — even to themselves.

## Theme 2: Pricing Is the Universal Alibi — But It Doesn't Hold Up

Pricing comes up in every single interview as a reason trials don't convert. Teachers say it unprompted, return to it when other explanations run out, and use it to close off inquiry. Yet when pressed, the evidence is almost entirely circumstantial: no teacher can recall a parent saying "the fee is too high" during the trial itself.

**What they say:**

- T01: "I feel it is the fee structure." — Then: "No one had discussed with me about this. This is my assumption based on the trial sessions based on the parent discussion." The price was never raised in-session. T01 had taken 9–10 trials with zero conversions.
- T04: "Most of my trials are rejected because the pricing, sir." — Then describes parents who check timings and dates, seem satisfied, and disappear. No pricing conversation happened in-trial.
- T06: "If they are not opting this or they are not enrolling their child, there could be a reason that they feel that the fees." — Immediately acknowledges: "people don't want to call that out explicitly."
- T07: "At the end once we share these levels and explain to them they are quickly jumping to only the fees and other things."

**Who showed it**: T01, T03, T04, T05, T06, T07, T09 — seven of ten.

**The structural problem with the alibi**: T02 is the clearest counterpoint. She describes a parent who raised pricing during the trial — and still enrolled, paying $18–20 per class. She reframes pricing questions as value conversations and says she's done it repeatedly. T05 (10/10 conversion) barely mentions pricing as a factor. T10 (very high conversion) is the only one who engages directly with the pricing question — and she contextualizes it for parents by comparing it to Sydney restaurant prices and making it tangible.

**What this theme actually reveals**: Pricing is the explanation teachers reach for when they don't have another. It is the gap-filler. The more a teacher has to say about what they do in trials, the less they mention pricing. The less they have to say, the more pricing fills the space. Teachers with low conversion rates are more likely to attribute failure to pricing; teachers with high conversion rates mention it as a thing they handle, not a brick wall.

## Theme 3: The Knowledge Gap Is Real — and Teachers Know It — But They Underestimate Its Trial Impact

Every teacher with an international license acknowledged some form of contextual knowledge gap. The gap is about local school systems, exam names, curriculum terminology, and grade-level conventions (year vs. grade). Teachers know the gap exists. What they don't recognize is the degree to which parents are actively testing for it — using curriculum questions as confidence proxies, not just information requests.

**What they say:**

- T02: "When I switch from US to UK region... they follow a year level stuff. Year five in UK is grade four in the other regions. So that part happened to be very confusing for me at the beginning... after interacting with one or two parents I got an idea."
- T08: "If you say naplan then you should be aware what is covered in naplan or maybe basics of naplan... if you are confident in telling those things right away in the trial, they get very confident. Okay, the teacher knows everything about those things. Now let's move on and see how she handles my child."
- T09: "Many of the teachers who are teaching UK may not even be aware that there is something called even 7 plus... it's only when you start teaching there that we come to know."
- T04: "I have a student with me from past three, four years. He craved the 11 plus exam... her mother used to sit with him like for three hours daily. And she was sending me some questions which are not covered here in QMAT syllabus."
- T01 (Whisper artifact in the original: "QMAS tutor" is consistently transcribed for "Cuemath tutor"): "I'm not getting any email. I'll be getting a WhatsApp message — the basic detail like the child name, the date and the thing and their expectation."

**Who showed it**: T01, T02, T04, T05, T06, T08, T09, T10 — eight of ten, with varying depth.

**Counter-evidence and nuance**: T03 is almost dismissive — "the syllabus is almost same like when we were trained, we were trained on the US syllabus" — but she primarily teaches Indian students and has limited exposure to ROW families. T07 says she knows IB terminology "PYP, FYP" and seems confident, but her student count is five and she admits trials have been dropping. T10 is the most contextually sophisticated teacher in the sample: she distinguishes ACARA from Victorian curriculum, explains why a parent from West Australia produced a curriculum not in the dropdown, and has independently figured out things from cluster director conversations.

**The insight the teachers are not fully articulating**: Parents aren't asking curriculum questions to get information. They already know (roughly) what they're studying. They ask to see if the teacher knows. It's a confidence test. T08 gets closest to naming this explicitly: "if you are confident in telling those things right away in the trial, they get very confident. Okay, the teacher knows everything... Now let's move on and see how she handles my child."

## Theme 4: The Trial Is Actually About the Parent, Not the Student — and Most Teachers Know This But Still Teach the Child

There is a near-universal recognition that the trial's real audience is the parent, not the student. But the way teachers describe their trials shows that most of them continue to direct most of their energy at the child, with parent management treated as secondary or as something that "happens" at the end.

**What they say:**

- T02: "For the entire class, the motive has to be the child has to learn something at the same point in time they shouldn't be pressured." — This is child-centric. The parent management she describes (stopping the parent from interrupting) is reactive, not planned.
- T05: "I always try to keep 15 minutes at the end so that I can answer all my questions [parent's questions]." — But what happens if the student takes longer than expected? The parent buffer is at risk.
- T09: "Talking to the parent is very crucial. Yes. That is the most important thing I believe. Yes. And what is happening is talking to the parent is at the end of the demo. At the end of the demo, they automatically shut off." — She identifies the structural problem with the trial format itself: the most important conversation happens at the end, when the platform terminates.
- T10: "I ask parents, tell me about yourself, tell me about your kid. So there they brief me." — She front-loads the parent engagement before the teaching starts. This is the most deliberate approach in the sample.
- T08: "With the first few seconds of the conversation, you actually understand whether the parent is actually interested in enrolling... parents are not interested, they'll be like yeah the kid is doing good and they won't elaborate."

**Who showed it**: T02, T05, T07, T08, T09, T10 — the parent-as-real-audience insight is present in most transcripts. But only T09, T10, and T02 demonstrate they have internalized it into their actual trial behavior.

**Counter-evidence**: T01 focuses almost entirely on teaching the child — her description of a trial is about accuracy, speed, concept explanations, going through sheets. The parent is peripheral in her account. She has zero conversions.

**The structural tension**: The trial format (as multiple teachers describe it) sequences the content delivery first and the parent conversation last. This is backward relative to the actual decision-making process. T09 is the only one who names this explicitly as a structural problem with the trial design, not just with how teachers run it.

## Theme 5: Teachers Want a Community — and Grieve the One That Was Taken Away

This theme emerged unexpectedly across multiple interviews. Teachers describe informal peer networks that used to function as a knowledge-sharing system — WhatsApp groups, informal Q&A, experienced teachers answering newer teachers' questions. These were shut down. The loss is felt practically (nowhere to ask questions) and emotionally (loss of connection).

**What they say:**

- T05: "There were some groups of teachers. So there were many teachers who had an idea that there was a program. They were quite helpful. They were not told by teachers, they would discuss with each other... they were very helpful. I just went through these sheets and saw what would happen... But now that is all closed, so maybe..." — The trailing off is notable. She doesn't finish the sentence.
- T05 again: "No, no, all the books were closed. If something had happened, then all the groups were closed. All the groups were closed... there were many questions and answers. So the teachers would put questions in the group. And the teachers were so helpful that they would answer the questions."
- T05 (on what a replacement might look like): "If there is a community where you can just connect, that would be good for you. Not WhatsApp, because WhatsApp is a little unorganized. But if there is a social media, why don't you make it an internal one? Where teachers can come and interact and share knowledge."
- T02 (describing WhiteHat Junior): "A regular personalized feedback on what works and what doesn't work helps us a lot... the trial classes were monitored... they were able to pinpoint at this particular point in time, you could have done this better."

**Who showed it**: T02, T05 (most explicit), T09 (implies it: "it's only when you start teaching there that we come to know" — no systematic handover). T07 references it obliquely when she says new teachers would benefit from the kind of experience she accumulated over eight years.

**Counter-evidence**: Some teachers (T03, T04, T06, T08, T10) don't mention peer communities at all. This may reflect recency (newer teachers like T08, T10 never had access to the groups so don't know to miss them) or individual preference.

**What makes this significant**: The community was doing work that no formal system was doing — distributing contextual knowledge about exams, curriculum, parent types. Its closure left a gap. Teachers are now solving this individually and inconsistently. T05 explicitly had to teach herself by going through old WhatsApp threads. T02 learned about UK curriculum from parents, not training.

## Theme 6: Trial Platform Bugs Are Actively Costing Conversions — and Teachers Are Absorbing the Cost

Multiple teachers describe specific technical problems with the trial interface that undermine the experience for parents and students. These are not vague complaints; they are named, specific failures. The striking thing is that teachers describe working around them in real-time, performing improvisations that should not be necessary, and then not knowing whether the failures have been reported or fixed.

**What they say:**

- T01: "When I'm showing the chapters the video within that question is not being played for the parent side... I have raised an issue in the not numbing actually [Whisper artifact — likely 'Noomi' or the feedback form system]. I had already informed but... it's frequently being done."
- T04: "I notice that when we started the trial and even it is written here, the same thing is shared at the student side. But when we start any like simulation... whatever maybe something else the child is seeing there and I'm seeing the different thing. So I'm not able to judge what actually is going on at child's interface."
- T06: "During the demo session the curriculum was UK curriculum. It was not mentioned in the WhatsApp message. So I was not aware... UK curriculum was not there. It was not there. I chose QMAT international board."
- T09: "Sometimes the student is also not unable to start, even if we are starting it on our own, they cannot do it from their end. So that one glitch which is happening in the initial part of the trial only is one unnecessary, not required glitch... in the beginning of the trial. The first thing that ice breaking part only there, there's a glitch."
- T09 on trial termination: "What is happening is talking to the parent is at the end of the demo. At the end of the demo, they automatically shut off... then whether the AC gets back to the parent or not, we do not know."
- T10: "The curriculum sometime is not listed what they were saying... Victorian curriculum... one year less... I had to pick the Australian curriculum." Also: "puzzles are there... now it should also be like students say like ma'am you did in the trial, puzzle. Now I have not got a lot of research. Moving sticks and making something out of it... I can't find that puzzle anywhere."

**Who showed it**: T01, T04, T06, T09, T10 — five teachers with specific technical failure observations. T08 alludes to platform issues more generally.

**Nuance**: Teachers are not collectively griping. They describe working around these problems — T06 chose a substitute curriculum board, T09 manually writes on screen when the student can't interact, T01 skips the video and explains verbally. The workarounds are functional in the moment but hide the problem from parents and from Cuemath — parents see a teacher who "seems to know how things work," not a platform with a broken ice-breaker.

**The unreported problem**: Several of these issues have apparently been reported (T01: "I had already informed"), and at least one (the trial timeout) seems to have been fixed. But teachers don't know what's been fixed and what hasn't. There is no feedback loop.

## Theme 7: The Conversion Algorithm Creates Anxiety and Perceived Unfairness — Especially for New Teachers

Multiple teachers are aware that trial allocation is driven by a conversion score algorithm. The mechanism produces frustration and anxiety, particularly among newer teachers whose scores are low simply because they've had fewer opportunities. The policy of single-region licenses compounds this — teachers feel capped on earning potential and unable to build a student base quickly enough.

**What they say:**

- T01: "I'm not getting any email. I'll be getting a WhatsApp message the basic detail like the child name, the date and the thing." — On being told the algorithm allocates to higher TC scorers: no reaction, she was apparently unaware.
- T05: "You won't believe. There was a time I was earning good. Now my earnings have been slashed out... earlier you asked us to go and earn more. Now you're asking to go down, slash everything. How is it fair? It's not fair." — This is T05's most emotionally charged moment in the transcript.
- T05 on the single-region policy: "I don't want to change my license. I want to add one more license... because earlier I was taking APAC. I have APAC students as well."
- T09: "The restriction may the problem is they are not realizing that if a teacher is available in particular sector timings only I cannot give my slot till midnight and three o'clock in the morning. That is not feasible for me."
- T08: "I was expecting at least like two to three trials per day because at WhiteHat we used to get two to three trials a day... APAC teachers, they said even in six months, they got like seven to eight trials."
- T07: "The conversion rate has dropped... you need to take more number of trials to get students earlier." — She notes this is a systemic observation, not just personal.

**Who showed it**: T01 (unaware), T05 (explicit grievance), T08 (expectation gap), T09 (constraint), T07 (systemic observation). T04, T10 don't raise it directly.

**Counter-evidence**: T02 converted at roughly 1-in-2 to 1-in-3 historically. T05 claims 10/10 conversion. Neither mentions the algorithm as a constraint. The algorithm presumably works in their favor.

**What makes this significant**: The algorithm creates a self-reinforcing disadvantage for new or returning teachers. A teacher like T06 (returning after health issues, limited availability) or T08 (one month in, low trial volume) is structurally prevented from building the score that would give them more trials, which would build the score. This is a negative flywheel. The teachers feel it but don't always have the vocabulary to articulate the mechanism — T08 knows something is off but thinks it might be the region or the school term.

## Theme 8: Motivation Is More Complex Than Money — But Money Pressure Is Real and Rising

No teacher leads with money as their primary motivation. Most invoke a version of love of teaching, connection with children, or personal identity as educators. But when income comes up — which it does in almost every interview — there is visible stress underneath.

**What they say:**

- T01: "If I have some independence on economic wise I feel like I'll be happy... I need some permanent job and I need an online job where I could take over like carry both my career as well as my family."
- T02: "I love interacting with children... it's not just a job for me." — But also has 15–20 students, two businesses, and describes her day in terms of managing income streams.
- T05: "I don't like the slot that gets empty... if that doesn't happen, I feel like I have to take it and fill it." — A revealing formulation. The drive is framed as psychological but the structure is economic.
- T05 (most direct): "My earnings have been slashed out... how is it fair?"
- T08: "I want to enroll a lot of students because that was my expectation from QMAT... I'm not yet received salary so you don't know exactly how much it will be. But yeah, I'm predicting."
- T09: "I just want to tell that why is that. Because earlier you asked us to go and earn more. Now you're asking to go down, slash everything. How is it fair?"

**Who showed it**: T01, T02, T05, T08, T09 — most explicitly. T06 mentions teaching as her passion and clearly loves it, but the subtext of her limited availability is income anxiety around the penalty system.

**The structure of the tension**: Teachers are expected to be intrinsically motivated educators, and they are. But they're also gig workers whose income depends on enrollment volume, trial conversion scores, and policy decisions made above them. The single-region restriction, the algorithm, and the penalty system all hit them economically in ways that conflict with the motivational narrative Cuemath expects them to project in trials. A teacher who is anxious about earnings is not in an optimal psychological state to project confidence to a parent.

## Theme 9: WhiteHat Junior Is the Implicit Benchmark — and Cuemath Consistently Falls Short on Training Infrastructure

Five teachers in this sample previously worked at WhiteHat Junior. All five reference their WhiteHat training as significantly better than what Cuemath provides. The comparison is not hostile — these teachers generally prefer Cuemath's product and values — but the operational and training infrastructure gap is stated plainly.

**What they say:**

- T02: "At WhiteHat Junior, the classes were monitored and propped out and we had to deliver a class in certain way... we used to get feedback for the trial classes as well and for the paid classes as well... they were able to pinpoint at this particular point in time, you could have done this better."
- T02 on current state: "Here we get feedback like once in a while, once in one or two months, that too of state class."
- T08: "I was working at WhiteHat Junior. So I was teaching coding and also by just Future School for Maths... at WhiteHat we used to get two to three trials a day." — Implies an expectation of trial volume and feedback cadence that Cuemath doesn't meet.
- T09: "I remember when I had joined, we used to be given a few videos, maybe not full trial, but at least a little snippets were there locally."
- T10: "In WhiteHat GR it was necessary because it's a coding platform. Training is must... But this is a maths platform... I don't think we need a training of doing algebra, doing fractions..." — Then immediately: "But then training would be required if you are not able to convert trials." She self-corrects and concedes the gap.
- T10 on onboarding: "The first two sessions of my training, very, very honest. Like the teacher was not ready to tell me anything... she was literally not interested in telling me anything. I was just... so what happens like my two sessions went like very lame."

**Who showed it**: T02, T08, T09, T10 explicitly. T05 (also WhiteHat background) implies it through her description of what good peer learning looked like before groups were closed.

**Counter-evidence**: T10 also argues that math doesn't need the same level of platform training as coding — a reasonable point. T04 (nine years at Cuemath) doesn't reference WhiteHat and seems to have built her own trial methodology through experience.

**What this theme reveals operationally**: WhiteHat trained teachers to convert trials, not just to teach. Cuemath trains teachers to teach. These are different skills and the gap shows up in conversion rates.

## Theme 10: The Trial Format Itself Is Contested — and a Prior Format Is Actively Missed

Multiple experienced teachers compare the current trial format unfavorably to a previous version. The previous format apparently allowed teachers to navigate the live Cuemath platform with the student — showing the actual product. The current format uses a separate trial-specific experience with puzzles and fixed question sets. Teachers argue the current format hides the product's strengths.

**What they say:**

- T09: "I personally feel the previous model of giving the trials was much more better. It was helping the student also in the sense that they could see the fun element of the platform when they click on check, you know, and they get their perfect hits."
- T09: "In this system, first we start with that one sort of a puzzle, like, you know, just what you say, to break the ice. Sometimes it doesn't start, frankly speaking. Sometimes the student is also not unable to start... that one glitch which is happening in the initial part."
- T07: "Earlier in the previous version of the demo we used to give like that [learning plan]. Maybe if that is given I think it would be better."
- T04: "I always like wanted that there must be Q board activities... we can't showcase that so many tools are there and we use them for building concept. That must be there."
- T09: "The student cannot write most of the time on that... it becomes again another [problem]... they are seeing the platform as is in the trial session and expecting that is how it is going to be in the actual class. But actually that is not so."

**Who showed it**: T04, T07, T09 most explicitly. T10 mentions puzzles that the student wanted to revisit after enrollment but couldn't find. T08 mentions that games and puzzles are good but lagging for lower grades.

**Counter-evidence**: The interviewer implies the current format was introduced for reasons (likely screen-sharing problems in the old format). T09 acknowledges this: "There must be a reason they won't just change." Teachers don't have visibility into why the format changed.

## What Surprised Me Most

**1. The zero-conversion teacher (T01) attributes everything to price — but has not had a single in-trial pricing conversation.** This is the starkest example of the alibi pattern. T01 has taken 9–10 trials, converted zero, and confidently explains this through pricing — while simultaneously revealing she has never heard the word "fee" during a trial. She describes parents who are "very happy," who say they'll "contact the academy counsellor we will join immediately by next week," and then disappear. She doesn't ask what happened to them. The dissonance between her confidence in the pricing explanation and the absence of any evidence for it is striking.

**2. The most operationally sophisticated teacher (T09) thinks the trial format is broken — and she converts.** T09 has been with Cuemath five years, teaches multiple geographies, has strong trial experience, and converts well. She is also the teacher with the most detailed critique of the trial platform — the ice-breaker glitch, the trial termination mid-parent-conversation, the student write-access problem. The fact that someone with this experience level still struggles with these issues suggests the problems are systemic, not skill-dependent.

**3. T10 describes an onboarding session where the trainer taught her nothing — and she names this as a concrete loophole.** The training infrastructure failure isn't abstract; there is at least one trainer who conducted sessions that left teachers without functional knowledge of the platform. T10 only got up to speed because a friend taught her what the trainer didn't.

**4. T05 is the highest-converting teacher in the sample (claims 10/10) — and is also the most economically frustrated.** The teacher who converts best is also the one who is angriest about earnings. The single-region restriction has cut her income. This should matter: if the system's best converters are also the most economically precarious, that's a retention risk.

**5. The curriculum gap is geographic and grade-specific in ways nobody has mapped.** T04 describes Singapore students leaving because the Cuemath curriculum is too easy for Grade 5. T09 explains that 11-plus preparation needs to start in Year 3, not Year 5. T10 describes the Victorian curriculum being one year behind Australian curriculum and the dropdown not showing it correctly. These are not vague "curriculum gaps" — they are precise, grade-specific, geography-specific failures that experienced teachers have identified individually and nobody appears to have consolidated.

## Notable Silences

**1. Nobody talks about what they say to close a trial.** There is extensive discussion of how to teach the child, how to engage the parent, how to handle objections. But no teacher describes what they say or do in the final five minutes to move a parent toward enrollment. T05 is the closest — she says she keeps 15 minutes for parent Q&A — but doesn't describe what she does with that time. This is arguably the most important moment in the trial and it is entirely absent from the transcripts.

**2. Nobody mentions having watched another teacher's trial.** Given that the company has hundreds of recorded trials, and that T09 explicitly recommends using trial recordings for training, it's striking that no teacher mentions having been shown a good trial recording as part of their development. The resource exists; no teacher has benefited from it.

**3. Nobody describes a structured CNA (Customer Needs Analysis) process.** The interviewer references a CNA form. Most teachers describe asking the parent what they want at the start of the trial. But this is ad hoc — each teacher has a different approach, asks different questions, reads the parent differently. There is no shared framework for the diagnostic conversation that precedes the teaching. T10 gets closest to a structured approach but learned it from WhiteHat and Bizohu [Whisper artifact — likely "Byju's"], not Cuemath.

**4. Nobody mentions Cuemath's methodology or pedagogy as a selling point in the trial.** The Cuemath Way, FUAR+GRIC, the conceptual learning approach — none of these are mentioned by any teacher as something they highlight to parents. Teachers talk about "structured curriculum," "practice sheets," and "the platform" — but not the pedagogical philosophy that differentiates Cuemath. This is either because the methodology training doesn't translate into trial conversations, or because teachers don't believe parents care about it.

**5. Nobody discusses how they handle a student who is clearly at the wrong level.** T05 alludes to it — she always keeps sheets at intermediate to prevent the child from getting stuck. But what happens when a student shows up and clearly needs to be three grades lower? Or is so advanced the trial content is boring? This is a live problem in trials (T04 describes Singapore students who found the content too easy and left) but no teacher has a clear approach to managing it in the moment.

**6. No teacher mentions the parent's post-trial experience** — what happens between the end of the trial and the admission counsellor call. Several teachers observe that parents who seemed enthusiastic during the trial don't convert afterward. But none describes any follow-up, any message, any attempt to stay in the parent's awareness during that gap. The teacher's influence apparently ends when the trial window closes.

## Summary: What This Teacher Population Is Experiencing

These ten teachers represent a wide spectrum — from a one-month newcomer with two students to a nine-year veteran with thirty, from zero conversions to self-reported 10/10. What they share is a common structural situation: they are skilled educators operating a gig work model with high stakes per trial, limited systematic support, and a training infrastructure that was always sparse and is now further reduced by the removal of peer community networks. Most of them have developed workarounds — personal research habits, informal contacts, post-hoc frameworks — that partially substitute for the missing institutional knowledge. These workarounds are idiosyncratic and non-transferable. The population is also collectively uncertain about what converts trials, using pricing as an explanatory default that satisfies psychologically but doesn't hold up analytically. The more experienced and higher-converting teachers in this sample share a common trait: they have figured out that the trial is fundamentally about the parent's confidence in the teacher, not about the child's performance in the session. They manage parents proactively, not reactively. But they arrived at this understanding through experience, not training, and none of them can articulate it as a replicable framework. The system is, in effect, selecting for teachers who figure this out on their own and losing teachers who don't — and it is doing nothing systematic to accelerate that learning curve.
