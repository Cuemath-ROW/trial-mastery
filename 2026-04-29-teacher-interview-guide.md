# Teacher Interview Guide — Trial Mastery Validation
### 5–7 teacher conversations to validate the module's psychological redesign

---

## Purpose

The delivery redesign plan rests on four hypotheses about what's broken in how teachers use the module. This interview is designed to test those hypotheses against real behavior — not assumed behavior.

**The four hypotheses being tested:**

| # | Hypothesis | What would confirm it |
|---|---|---|
| H1 | Teachers can't connect their behavior to trial outcomes (competence visibility gap) | They attribute outcomes to the parent, not to what they did |
| H2 | Training content doesn't reach them at the moment of the trial (retrieval gap) | They do zero content preparation in the 30–60 min before a trial |
| H3 | Experienced teachers disengage from training as an identity threat | They see training as remedial, not as a tool for optimisation |
| H4 | No if-then commitments means no behavior change (implementation intentions gap) | They go into trials with no explicit behavioral intention set |

**Sample**: 5–7 active teachers. Minimum 2 trials in the last 60 days. Aim for a mix of experience levels — at least 2 with fewer than 15 trials total, at least 2 veterans (50+). Mix of conversion rates if accessible.

**Format**: 20–25 minute conversation. This is a guide, not a script — follow threads that open up. The questions are ordered to build trust before asking anything sensitive.

**How to open the conversation**:
> "I'm researching how we can make the trial experience better — not just for parents, but for you as a teacher. I want to understand what's actually going on from your side: what you're thinking before a trial, how you make decisions during it, and what happens afterwards. There are no right answers here. The more honest you are, the more useful this is for both of us."

---

## The Questions

### Section 1 — Background (calibrate experience and self-perception)

**Q1** *(Structured)*
How long have you been taking trials, and roughly how many have you done in total?

*What you're measuring*: Experience tier. Use this to interpret every other answer — a veteran's "I just go in and see what happens" means something different from a new teacher's.

---

**Q2** *(Open)*
If you had to estimate — what percentage of your trials result in a parent enrolling? 1 in 2? 1 in 3?

*Follow-up if they're not sure*: "That's fine — just your gut feel."

*What you're measuring*: Self-awareness of actual conversion rate. If you have their real rate, note the gap. A large gap (thinks 50%, actually 25%) is a direct Dunning-Kruger indicator — they have no honest mirror.

---

### Section 2 — Pre-trial preparation (test retrieval and commitment gaps)

**Q3** *(Open)*
Walk me through what you do in the 30–60 minutes before a trial starts. What are you doing, what are you thinking about?

*What you're measuring*: Is there any deliberate content preparation? Or is preparation purely logistical (checking timezone, opening the app, confirming the link)? Teachers who do zero content review before going in confirm the retrieval gap — training reached them weeks ago, not at the point of execution.

*Probes if needed*: "Do you think about anything specific you want to make sure you do in that session?" / "Do you look anything up?"

---

**Q4** *(Specific)*
Have you ever gone back to any training material — a document, a module, anything from Cuemath — right before a trial, or after one that didn't go well?

*If yes*: "What did you look at? What triggered it?"
*If no*: "Is there anything you wish you could quickly refer to before you start?"

*What you're measuring*: Whether there's an existing pull-to-material behavior. If teachers already look things up before difficult trials, there's a habit we can amplify. If no one does — the retrieval gap is structural, not individual.

---

**Q5** *(Specific)*
Before a trial starts, do you ever set a specific intention for yourself — like "in this session, I want to make sure I do X"? Or do you go in and respond to what the parent brings?

*What you're measuring*: Whether implementation intentions exist organically. If some teachers do this intuitively, it validates the mechanism — we're just making it explicit and consistent. If no one does it, the gap is confirmed and the commitment card (T1-C) is necessary, not optional.

---

### Section 3 — In-trial experience (test competence visibility and behavior attribution)

**Q6** *(Behavioral)*
Tell me about a trial that went really well — one that converted, or one where you just felt confident the whole way through. What do you think you did in that session that made the difference?

*What you're measuring*: Can the teacher name a specific behavior they did — a question they asked, a technique they used, a moment they handled well? Or do they attribute the outcome to the parent ("she was already looking to enroll"), to luck, or to generic factors ("I was relaxed that day")?

*This is the most important question in the interview.* If teachers can't name a specific behavior they can take credit for, the competence signal is absent — and without it, motivation to improve has no anchor.

---

**Q7** *(Behavioral)*
Now tell me about a trial that didn't convert — one where you think something could have gone differently. What do you think happened?

*Follow-up*: "Did you do anything differently in your next trial because of that?"

*What you're measuring*: Whether failure generates behavioral learning. If the attribution is consistently external ("parent wasn't serious", "wrong timing", "the child was distracted"), the feedback loop isn't closing even when outcomes are bad. If they changed something afterward, that's a working learning loop we can systematise.

---

**Q8** *(Specific)*
When a parent raises an objection during a trial — like "I need to think about it" or "it's more than I expected to spend" — what do you typically do in that moment?

*What you're measuring*: Direct behavioral test of Part C content (objection handling). Note whether their approach is improvised, framework-based, or whether any module language is visible in how they describe it. Don't prompt them with the framework — let them describe their actual behavior.

---

### Section 4 — Motivation and trial allocation (test competence visibility lever)

**Q9** *(Open)*
Beyond the income from teaching, what's your main motivation for taking trials? What makes a week feel good to you?

*What you're measuring*: The dominant motivation type — identity ("I want to be a good teacher"), instrumental ("I want more paid students"), or relational ("I like meeting parents"). This determines which framing will land. Teachers driven by identity respond to the "be the best" frame; income-optimisers respond to the TCScore allocation frame.

---

**Q10** *(Specific)*
Do you have a sense of how Cuemath decides which teachers get the next available trial? How does that work, as far as you understand it?

*What you're measuring*: Awareness of the TCScore mechanism. Most teachers believe trials are random or assigned by rotation. If that belief is widespread across 5–7 teachers, the T1-B static slide (honest TCScore explanation) is confirmed as high-leverage — the motivation to convert more already exists, but the causal link to trial allocation is missing.

*Do not explain TCScore here — just note their understanding and move on.*

---

**Q11** *(Hypothetical)*
If you knew — for certain — that improving your conversion rate by 10–15 percentage points would move you to the top of the trial allocation queue, meaning you'd get the next available trial before other teachers — would that change how you prepare or approach trials?

*If yes*: "What would you do differently?"
*If no or unsure*: "What would be more motivating for you?"

*What you're measuring*: Responsiveness to loss aversion / allocation framing once the mechanism is made explicit. If teachers say "yes, absolutely" — the TCScore bridge (T1-B) will work as a motivational lever. If they're indifferent — either income from trials isn't their primary driver, or they believe they're already at maximum effort.

---

### Section 5 — Training and the module (test identity threat and module utility)

**Q12** *(Open)*
Have you used the Trial Mastery module, or any Cuemath training for trials? What do you actually remember from it — and has anything from it changed how you run a trial?

*If they've done it*: "What one thing from it sticks with you? Did anything shift after you went through it?"
*If they haven't*: "What would make you want to use something like that?"

*What you're measuring*: Recall and transfer. If they completed the module but remember almost nothing specific — knowledge decay is confirmed (Ebbinghaus). If they remember content but don't use it in trials — it's a retrieval and transfer gap. If they avoided it — it may be identity threat or simple time pressure.

---

**Q13** *(Closing — open)*
Last question. If you could change one thing about how you go into or come out of a trial — something you wish you knew beforehand, or something you wish happened differently — what would it be?

*What you're measuring*: Unprompted desires. Whatever surfaces here is something the teacher is already motivated to want — the module just needs to deliver it. If 4 of 7 teachers say the same thing, that's the most important section to fix or the most important mechanism to add.

## Synthesis — What to Look For Across All 7 Teachers

After all conversations, map each teacher's notes to the four hypotheses. The patterns that matter:

**Pattern A — Attribution gap (H1)**
*Signal*: 5+ teachers attribute trial outcomes to parent readiness or luck rather than to a specific behavior they controlled.
*Implication*: The competence visibility mechanism (T2-A: live TCScore data) is the single highest-leverage intervention. Without it, every other change is slower-acting.

**Pattern B — Preparation vacuum (H2)**
*Signal*: 5+ teachers do zero content preparation in the period immediately before a trial.
*Implication*: The pre-trial brief card (T1-D) is confirmed as high-impact. The T2-C notification deep-link becomes urgent — teachers won't seek it out, so it has to arrive at them.

**Pattern C — TCScore ignorance + high responsiveness (H1 + Q11)**
*Signal*: Most teachers don't know about TCScore (Q10), but say "yes, I'd change my approach" to Q11.
*Implication*: The motivation exists. The information is missing. The T1-B static TCScore bridge will close the gap without any API integration. High confidence, low effort.

**Pattern D — Module dropout or low transfer (H3)**
*Signal*: Teachers completed the module but can't name a specific technique they now use in trials.
*Sub-signal A — recall but no transfer*: They remember the content but don't use it → retrieval and transfer gap. SRS flashcards + pre-trial brief are the fix.
*Sub-signal B — didn't complete or start*: → Identity threat or motivation gap. The identity frame (T1-A) and TCScore bridge (T1-B) need to precede all content.

**Pattern E — Common gap from Q13**
*Signal*: 3+ teachers name the same unprompted desire (e.g., "I wish I knew how to handle parents who say they need to think about it").
*Implication*: That is the most important section to convert to scenario-based format (T1-F priority). Their unprompted desire reveals where the training-to-behavior gap is felt most acutely.
